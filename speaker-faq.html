1. What is TED? What is TEDx?

TED is an organization that holds conferences where people from diverse backgrounds come and talk about their ideas. TEDx is an event modeled on the TED-style of conferences but is organized independently by individuals or organizations.



2. What is the TED Talk format? What is a TEDx event like?

The TED Talk format has one speaker speaking about their idea on stage for around 12 minutes. The talk can be a maximum of 18 minutes and the shorter talks usually go for around 12 minutes. There is usually no Question-Answer session, however, the attendees can interact with the speakers during the breaks.

A TEDx event generally consists of several such talks by people from different fields (such as sociologists, engineers, sportspersons, architects, social workers, scientists, etc). The talks take place one after the other with some breaks and performances in between. The purpose is to expose the audience to the new or less discussed ideas of the speakers. A TEDx event is a platform for telling people about ideas that are worth spreading.

Please note that a TEDx event happens once in a year, and all the speakers come and speak on that day alone. This is not a talk series.

3. What is IISER Pune and where is it?

Indian Institute of Science Education and Research (IISER) Pune is a premier science research institute in Pashan, Pune. The IISERs are to scientific research what the IITs are to engineering. IISER Pune has an active research community and a scientifically inclined student population ranging from undergraduate to postgraduate. Here is the link to our website.

5. What is to be spoken? Who will be there to hear it? What is the theme? How do I (the speaker) come in here?

At TEDx, we search year-round for speakers who will inform and inspire, surprise and delight. TEDx talks are created in the spirit of TED’s overall mission to research and discover “ideas worth spreading.” So we want you to interpret our theme: ‘Frosted Glass’ in terms of your work and idea to shape your talk. To find out more about the theme, go to Q7. We will be assisting you in curating an inspiring talk that inculcates our theme amicably.

That said there are a couple of constraints to keep in mind while creating the talk. First, the time limit which we have already discussed. The second is the audience. The audience for a TEDx event is a very diverse audience with people from a variety of places coming to attend. For TEDxIISERPune specifically, we expect a significant number of our audience to be coming from the scientific research complex that we live in (around 60%). The other 40% we expect will be from the general audience from all over India. Thirdly, TED has some content guidelines that need to be followed.

7. But what is the theme?

There are many things that ‘Frosted Glass’ represents: a hindrance to clarity, a way to conceal the truth, an illusion of reality, maybe a new perspective to life. Our society thrives in a fog of misconceptions and a haze of reality. The most elementary ideas, like the concept of sexuality as a spectrum, are still unclear to many. Through our theme, we wish to highlight issues like the misinterpretation of ideologies around feminism, anti-casteism, anti-colorism, pride, and more. We also want to highlight the propagation of half-baked interpretations made by the media.

Our theme also aims at presenting the various efforts that scientists, philosophers, and innovators are making to explore the other side of the “Frosted Glass”. Science is constantly probing fundamental questions, trying to understand the true nature of the universe better. Meanwhile, philosophy is trying to question the methodology of scientific observation. Innovators and entrepreneurs are relentlessly working to improve our lives; starting from seemingly absurd and vague ideas and building upon them while overcoming many hurdles that cross their paths.

The very notion of science explores the hazy ideas prevalent in the world that need more concrete descriptions or explanations. So, science is a medium that explores misconceptions and replaces them with facts. It offers a different but correct perspective of viewing phenomenons, societal or natural, that are in dire need of valid interpretations. The world beyond the haziness is a world that science has yet to explore, but the incessant efforts of the scientists to curb the haziness can become an inspiration to overcome the social prejudice that exists due to incorrect perspectives.

Frosted Glass also highlights the false notions regarding mental health and other psychological issues, the difference between sexuality and gender, the distinction between science and pseudoscience, and many more. These wrong notions exist due to false and incomplete representations of these issues. The media does an excellent job of twisting facts, swaying the public away from reality. Lack of reliable sources is a ginormous “frosted glass”.

Frosted Glass offers us an opportunity to look at life through a different lens. Like our dreams that challenge the linearity of events, having a hazy vision of the future provides us an opportunity to let our imagination run wild. A lot of teenagers entering society are unclear of the path they should be opting. We want to encourage people not to look at the “frosted glass” as a hindrance but rather as the freedom to shape our future.

Thus, “Frosted Glass” represents all the fuzziness prevalent in our perceptions, thoughts, and actions. It aims at highlighting common misconceptions and obscured views present around us as a society, while it also goes about how we can use a fogged view of something to further build upon it and shape it the way we wish it to be.



8. What can this Talk be like?

Talks come in several kinds and the one you choose to deliver depends upon your style of talking and your subject. You don’t have to worry about which category you will fall under at this point, our team will collaborate with you to shape your talk. But we want to give you an idea about the diversity of ways one can give a talk on the TEDx platform and also show you what is key to great talks.

  • The Big Idea

    Talks that take up one important idea or issue, which may be well known, and emphasize one or two very strong points.
    Examples: Bryan Stevenson, Onora O'Neill, Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

  • The Tech Demo

    Talks that involve presenting a new piece of technology that was made by the speaker. It may not be a demonstration but rather a portrayal of the idea behind the invention.
    Examples: Tan Le, Markus Fischer, Raffaello D'Andrea, Manu Prakash

  • The Artist’s Statement

    In these talks, artists showcase their art and explain the meaning and process behind what they create.
    Examples: Raghava KK, Liu Bolin, Aparna Rao

  • The Wow Talk

    These talks are mainly about the amazement of science and discovery.
    Examples: Yoav Medan, Marcus Byrne, Janna Levin

  • The Small Idea

    These talks are not about one big, world-changing idea, but instead a very engaging take on an interesting topic.
    Examples: Joe Smith, Mary Roach, Charlie Todd

  • The Issue Talk

    These talks expose your audience to an issue that they may not otherwise know much about.
    Examples: Rodrigo Canales, Lawrence Lessig, Rose George
